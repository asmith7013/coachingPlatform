Teacher/Student Strategies:

Suggested Strategy for an Initial Lesson: Benchmark Angles. Students can view a right angle as a benchmark, determining whether an angle is acute or obtuse by its size relative to a right angle.

Models and Manipulatives:

Highlight Model: Angles in the Real World. Have students identify line segments, rays, points, and different types of angles that they find in the classroom or at home. Ask students what angles they find at different times on a clock, or what angles they find between their desk and the floor.

Questions to Help Students Get Unstuck:

  • What attributes do all line segments have in common? What about rays?
  • Look at the shape. How many straight sides does it have?

Discussion Questions:

  • What information do you need in order to classify an object?
  • What kinds of angles make up a rectangle or square?
  • Is it possible for a shape to be made up of a point, a line segment, a ray, and a line? Explain.   

Common Misconceptions:

  • Students might confuse line segments, lines, and rays.
  • Students might not be aware of the fact that angles can be classified differently and might have trouble noticing nuances in their sizes.
